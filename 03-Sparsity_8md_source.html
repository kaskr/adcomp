3 Sparsity {#Sparsity}
4 ========
5 
6 Large random effect models require sparsity in order to work in TMB.
7 In this section we will discuss:
8 
9 - What exactly we mean by *sparsity*.
10 - How to formulate *sparse* models (the same model can sometimes be formulated as both dense and sparse).
11 - How to calculate the sparsity pattern of a given TMB model.
12 - How to visualize sparsity either as a matrix or a graph.
13 - How to use sparsity for general optimization problems (not just random effects).
14 
15 
16 ## Conditional independence graphs and DAGs
17 
18 ### Conditional independence
19 There are various graph representations that are commonly used to
20 visualize probabilistic structure. One such is the *conditional
21 independence graph*. Say we have a model of four random variables \f$X_1,...,X_4\f$ for which the joint density is:
22 
23 \f[p(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4) \propto f_1(x_1,x_2)f_2(x_2,x_3)f_3(x_3,x_4)f_4(x_4,x_1)\f]
24 
25 The separability of factors on the right hand side implies some conditional independence properties. For instance if \f$x_1\f$ and \f$x_3\f$ are held constant then \f$x_2\f$ and \f$x_4\f$ varies independently. We say that \f$x_2\f$ and \f$x_4\f$ are conditionally independent given \f$x_1\f$ and \f$x_3\f$.
26 The conditional independence graph is defined by drawing *undirected* edges between variables occurring in the same factor \f$f_i\f$:

44 ### Node removal properties
45 
46 Important probabilistic properties can be deduced directly from the graph. This is due to the following *node removal* properties.
47 
48 1. The conditional distribution given node \f$X_i\f$ is found by removing \f$X_i\f$ and its edges from the graph. For instance conditional on \f$X_4\f$ we get the following graph:
49  <div class="figure" style="text-align: center">
50  <img src="figure/unnamed-chunk-4-1.svg" alt="plot of chunk unnamed-chunk-4" />
51  <p class="caption">plot of chunk unnamed-chunk-4</p>
52  </div>
53 
54 2. The marginal distribution wrt. a node \f$X_i\f$ is found by removing \f$X_i\f$ from the graph and connecting *all* \f$X_i\f$'s neighbors. For instance when integrating \f$X_4\f$ out of the joint density we get the following graph for the remaining nodes:
55  <div class="figure" style="text-align: center">
56  <img src="figure/unnamed-chunk-5-1.svg" alt="plot of chunk unnamed-chunk-5" />

58  </div>
59 
60 > Conditioning preserves sparseness. Marginalizing tend to destroy sparseness by adding more edges to the graph.

62 ### Directed acyclic graph
63 
64 When building models in TMB it is often more natural to specify processes in incremental steps - i.e. through the successive conditional distributions. The previous example could be simulated by drawing the variables \f$X_1,X_2,X_3,X_4\f$ one by one in the given order as illustrated by the following *directed* graph:
65 
66 <div class="figure" style="text-align: center">
67 <img src="figure/unnamed-chunk-6-1.svg" alt="plot of chunk unnamed-chunk-6" />
68 <p class="caption">plot of chunk unnamed-chunk-6</p>
69 </div>
70 
71 The graph shows dependencies of any specific node *given past nodes*. The edge from \f$X_1\f$ to \f$X_3\f$ was not in the original (undirected) graph. This is a so-called **fill-in**.
72 
73 > Order matters. The DAG is different from the conditional independence graph.

95 ## The theta logistic example
96 
97 Consider the ``theta logistic'' population model [@pedersen2011estimation]. This is a
98 state-space model with state equation
99 
100 \f[u_t = u_{t-1} + r_0\left(1-\left(\frac{\exp(u_{t-1})}{K}\right)^\psi\right) + e_t\f]
101 
102 and observation equation
103 
104 \f[y_t = u_t + v_t\f]
105 
106 where \f$e_t \sim N(0,Q)\f$, \f$v_t \sim N(0,R)\f$ and \f$t\in \{0,...,n-1\}\f$. A
107 uniform prior is implicitly assigned to \f$u_0\f$.
108 
109 The joint negative log-likelihood of state vector \f$u\f$ and measurements
110 \f$y\f$ is implemented in the C++ template \ref thetalog.cpp.
111 The example can be run by:
112 
113 
114 ```r
115 runExample("thetalog", exfolder="adcomp/tmb_examples")
116 ```
117 
118 
119 
120 We demonstrate it in the case \f$n=5\f$. Here is the DAG
121

127 This is a standard hidden Markov structure. Each data node is bound to a *single* random effect - hence the data does not introduce additional edges in the random effect structure.
